Hypergraphs

The vertices and edges of our hypergraph are contained within some global, countably infinite set $\mca$ of atoms.

Definition 1 (Hypergraph)
Let a hypergraph with $m$ inputs and $n$ outputs comprise of

  • Two finite sets $\vt \subset \mca$ (target) and $\vs \subset \mca$ (source) of vertices

    • such that $|\vt| = |\vs|$ and $\vt \cap \vs = \emptyset$

    • equipped with total orders $\vtorder$ and $\vsorder$ respectively.

  • A finite set $E \subset \mca$ of edges and two extra edges $\einput$ (input) and $\eoutput$ (output), $\einput, \eoutput \not\in E$

  • Two functions

    • $\morph{\vleft \text{ (left)}}{\vt}{E \uplus \{\einput\}}$

      • with its inverse image $\morph{\etargets \text{ (targets)}}{E \uplus \{\einput\}}{\powerset^{\vtorder}(\vt)}$

      • such that $|\etargets(\einput)| = m$

    • $\morph{\vright \text{ (right)}}{\vs}{E \uplus \{\eoutput\}}$

      • with its inverse image $\morph{\esources \text{ (sources)}}{E \uplus \{\eoutput\}}{\powerset^{\vsorder}(\vs)}$

      • such that $|\esources(\eoutput)| = n$

  • A bijective function $\morph{\vconnsr \text{ (connections)}}{\vt}{\vs}$

  • A finite set of labels $L$ and a labelling function $\morph{\labels \text{ (labels)}}{E}{L}$

    • such that $\forall e_1, e_2 \in E$, if $\labels(e_1) = \labels(e_2)$ then

      • $|\esources(e_1)| = |\esources(e_2)|$

      • $|\etargets(e_1)| = |\etargets(e_2)|$

For a hypergraph with $m$ inputs and $n$ outputs, we write it as $\morph{f}{m}{n}$, where $m \to n$ is the type of the hypergraph. The pair of sources and targets of an edge $(\esources(e), \etargets(e))$ is referred to as its interface – if a vertex is a member of either of these sets we say that it is incident on edge $e$. The input and output edge $\einput$ and $\eoutput$ are ‘special’ edges that all hypergraphs have – they collect the ‘dangling’ vertices on the left and the right edges of the hypergraph. Subsequently $\etargets(\einput)$ and $\esources(\eoutput)$ can be thought of as the input and output ‘ports’ of the entire hypergraph. We sometimes refer to the elements of the set $E$ as the regular edges of the hypergraph.

The following is an example of a hypergraph, representing a flip-flop. The label $\nor$ represents a NOR gate and $\fork$ represents the forking of a wire.

[ex:hypergraph]
\[V^I = V^O = \{v_{0},v_{1},v_{2},v_{3},v_{4},v_{5},v_{6},v_{7}\}\]\[E = \{e_{0},e_{1},e_{2},e_{3}\}\]\[\lambda = \{v_{0} \mapsto \alpha,v_{1} \mapsto \alpha,v_{2} \mapsto e_{0},v_{3} \mapsto e_{1},v_{4} \mapsto e_{1},v_{5} \mapsto e_{2},v_{6} \mapsto e_{3},v_{7} \mapsto e_{3}\}\]\[\rho = \{v_{0} \mapsto e_{0},v_{1} \mapsto e_{0},v_{2} \mapsto e_{1},v_{3} \mapsto e_{2},v_{4} \mapsto e_{2},v_{5} \mapsto e_{3},v_{6} \mapsto \omega,v_{7} \mapsto \omega\}\]\[\kappa = \{v_{0} \mapsto v_{0},v_{1} \mapsto v_{4},v_{2} \mapsto v_{2},v_{3} \mapsto v_{6},v_{4} \mapsto v_{3},v_{5} \mapsto v_{5},v_{6} \mapsto v_{1},v_{7} \mapsto v_{7}\}\]\[L = \{\downarrow, \curlywedge\}\]\[\labels = \{e_{0} \mapsto \downarrow,e_{1} \mapsto \curlywedge,e_{2} \mapsto \downarrow,e_{3} \mapsto \curlywedge\}\]\[\esources = \{e_{0}\mapsto \{{v_{0}, v_{1}}\},e_{1}\mapsto \{{v_{2}}\},e_{2}\mapsto \{{v_{3}, v_{4}}\},e_{3}\mapsto \{{v_{5}}\},\omega\mapsto \{{v_{6}, v_{7}}\}\}\]\[\etargets = \{\alpha\mapsto \{{v_{0}, v_{1}}\},e_{0}\mapsto \{{v_{2}}\},e_{1}\mapsto \{{v_{3}, v_{4}}\},e_{2}\mapsto \{{v_{5}}\},e_{3}\mapsto \{{v_{6}, v_{7}}\}\}\]

This example can be seen graphically in Figure [fig:flipflop], drawn both in an informal and formal style. Informally the left and right maps are represented by wires from the left and right of the vertices, and the connections map is represented by these vertices sharing a point. When drawing hypergraphs formally, we stack the vertices up according to their order, with source vertices on the left and the target vertices on the right. In our notation we label vertices with a natural number, so the ordering is the ordering of the natural numbers. The edges are also stacked, although this order is irrelevant. The left ($\vleft$) and right ($\vright$) maps are drawn as arrows pointing left or right appropriately. For clarity, the connections ($\vconnsr$) map will generally be written shorthand as an arrow and the vertex number, rather than cluttering up the graph with many arrows.
